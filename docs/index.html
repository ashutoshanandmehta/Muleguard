Stolen money travels through ordinary-looking bank accounts.

When someone falls for an online scam, the stolen money doesn't go straight to the criminal. It first lands in a normal person's bank account which is called a money mule account, and within hours it is forwarded, split up into different accounts, and then withdrawn. Each account on its own looks innocent. MuleGuard AI looks at all accounts of a bank together, and the payments flowing between them, and gives every account a risk score so the bank's fraud team knows exactly which accounts to check first.

Fig. 1 — A typical mule chain: a victim's money enters a mule account, gets split across several pass-through accounts, and exits as cash, crypto, or UPI transfers. The mule account looks normal on its own and the pattern is visible only when you look at the network of payments and their timing.

Why it's hard

A mule account passes every normal check — be it a real person, real ID (KYC) or a normal balance. The giveaway is behaviour over time: money arrives, leaves within hours, splits across many accounts, and this repeats in bursts. To catch that, software must study the network of payments. It should not be studying accounts one by one, and it must not flood the fraud team with false alarms.

The proposed solution: MuleGuard AI

architecture

MuleGuard AI is a complete, working pipeline which extends from raw data to a ranked list of suspicious accounts on the fraud analyst's desk. Currently, it's a Python CLI (command-line) prototype. It has four stages:

Stage 1

Data

Realistic banking data from AMLSim (an open-source bank transaction simulator built by IBM, which includes labelled mule accounts), converted into MuleGuard's format. Four ready-made fraud scenario demos included: digital-arrest scam, phishing UPI, loan-app scam, betting/crypto.

convert_amlsimdata/scenarios
Stage 2

The map & the clues

Builds a "map" connecting accounts, customers, devices, IP addresses and merchants (who pays whom, who logs in from where). Alongside, it computes 42 behaviour clues per account — e.g. how fast money leaves after arriving, whether money moves in circles, whether the account sits in the middle of a payment chain.

graph_datasetbuild_features
Stage 3

Two models together

A classic model scores each account from its 42 clues; a graph neural network (GNN) starts from that score and adds the extra signal revealed from the payment network. Details below.

tabular_teachertrain_gnn
Stage 4

Decisions

Scores every account, ranks them, and exports: an alert list for analysts, a tamper-evident audit log, evaluation reports, a kill switch, and manual overrides.

evaluateoperationalalerts · audit

The classic model (the "teacher")

Gradient boosting — the workhorse of tabular machine learning, i.e. learning from spreadsheet-style data where each account is one row and each clue is one column. It scores each account from its 42 behaviour clues and sets the baseline.

The network model (the GNN)

A graph neural network learns from the payment map itself. It starts from the classic model's score and adds the extra signal revealed from the network — so the combined system gets better than the classic model.

How it was tested

methodology

The number that matters to a bank: if the fraud team only has time to review the top 5% riskiest accounts, how many of the real mules are in that pile? We call this the "catch rate at 5%". Everything below is about making that number trustworthy, not just big.

Step 1

Train on the past, test on the future

Accounts are ordered by time and the AMLSim dataset is split in a 60:20:20 ratio. The model learns from the oldest 60%, is tuned on the next 20%, and is judged on the newest 20%.

Step 2

No future data leakage

The classic model is trained only on the training window, and even there its scores are produced in a "hold-one-part-out" way, it never gets to grade rows it memorised.

Step 3

Start from the baseline, add only real signal

The GNN begins as an exact copy of the classic model's answer, and training can only add what the payment map genuinely reveals. If the map holds no extra information, the score stays where it was.

Step 4

Repeat and refresh

Every experiment runs with multiple random seeds (fresh restarts each time it runs).

Finding 1 — the idea works

The hybrid beat its own teacher in every repeated run.

Finding 2 — the practice data ran out of secrets

The improvement was small, and rebuilding the network in a smarter way (modelling each transaction with its exact timing) changed nothing. Reason: AMLSim is a simulator, and the 42 hand-made clues already describe almost everything the simulator generates — there was little left for the network model to discover. What's needed: data where the payment network still holds information the clues don't capture — bigger simulations with overlapping fraud patterns, or real bank data.

Finding 3 — reproducibility

By the project's own strict rule, the classic model remains the recommended choice on this dataset, and every experiment — including the failed ideas — is reproducible. (In real bank data, hand-made clues are rarely this perfect, which is exactly where the hybrid is designed to shine.)

Verdict · Needs richer data

The hybrid model needs richer data to shine.

Limitations

05 · stated, not hidden
  • Simulated data. AMLSim generates fraud using rules, and our clues mirror those rules — real-world results may differ in either direction.
  • Unequal comparison. The teacher model gets less training data (60%) than the standalone reference model (80%), so their scores aren't directly comparable.
  • Small demo dataset. The 1,000-account sample included in the repository is for checking that everything runs, but its test window has only 6 mule accounts — too few to measure quality. For better results, train on the 100K dataset.
  • Scale. The 100K dataset trains comfortably on a laptop; a 1-million-account bank would need the training to work in smaller batches.
  • One static snapshot. The model trains once on a fixed period. A production system would need to retrain on a rolling schedule.

Future enhancements

06 · in order of expected value
  • Richer data first. AMLSim's 1-million-account version, then real (anonymised) bank data, where the payment network still holds secrets the clues don't capture.
  • Rolling time windows. Train, evaluate and retrain the way a live bank would, if possible.
